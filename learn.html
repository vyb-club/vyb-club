Step-by-step view

A simple roadmap before you dive into terms

Think of financial products as tools. Each tool serves a different need: wealth creation, income stability, protection, or flexibility.

1

Growth Tools

Equity, equity funds, midcap, smallcap, flexicap, multicap, index funds, and ETFs are used when you want long-term growth.

2

Stability Tools

Debt, liquid funds, and corporate debt funds focus more on preserving capital and smoother returns than aggressive growth.

3

Diversification Tools

Commodities and REITs add exposure beyond shares and bonds, helping spread risk across different asset types.

4

Protection Tools

Health insurance, term insurance, and riders protect you from large financial shocks instead of trying to grow money.

When you buy equity, you become a part-owner of a business. If the company does well, your investment may grow. If it performs badly, the value can fall.

Debt investments work like you giving a loan to a government or company. In return, they usually pay interest and are generally less volatile than equity.

Commodities are physical goods used in the economy. People invest in them to diversify, hedge inflation, or benefit when prices of those goods rise.

Health insurance helps pay for hospitalization, treatments, and certain medical costs. It protects your savings from being wiped out by a large health emergency.

Term insurance pays a fixed amount to your nominee if you die during the policy term. It is meant for protection, not investment or returns.

Riders are extra benefits added to an insurance policy, such as critical illness cover, accidental death cover, or waiver of premium. They usually cost extra.

A lock-in period forces you to stay invested for a minimum time. It helps long-term discipline, but reduces flexibility because you cannot access the money easily.

Some mutual funds charge exit load if you redeem units before a specified period. It discourages short-term entry and exit by investors.

The expense ratio covers fund management and operating costs. It is deducted from the fund’s assets, so a higher ratio can reduce your long-term returns.

AUM stands for Assets Under Management. It shows the total value of money a fund or asset manager is handling on behalf of investors.

Volatility shows how sharply prices can rise or fall. Higher volatility can create higher return potential, but it also means the investment may feel uncomfortable in the short term.
